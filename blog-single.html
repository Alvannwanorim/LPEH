Energy use in Nigeria’s public sector

  • John Doe
  • Jan 1, 2020
  • 12 Comments

It is a known fact that Nigeria’s electricity generation has hovered around the 4,000MW mark for more than a decade. The industrial sector has generated power for itself through gas and diesel powered generators to sustain their operations. This is not easy task considering the high cost of self-generating and the fact that they have to stay competitive.

The focus in this article however, is rather on the public sector which comprises our public institutions and their respective buildings.

Nigeria’s public service has been viewed as a place where unserious and laid back employees will likely be found. Of course, this may be true but not absolutely. The reason why these views are seen in the general public is simply because of their attitude to work, inefficiencies and sometimes the mingling of partisan politics into what should be a value driven public service.

Lackadaisical and inefficient energy use in the public sector is also a very serious issue and of major concern. Most public buildings rarely pay electricity bills commensurate with what they actually consume. It’s either they do not pay electricity bills or they enjoy huge discounts. Interestingly, there are no measures in these buildings to keep energy consumption as efficient as possible. No sensitization, no awareness and most importantly, there is no obligation on the part of government employees to use energy efficiently. Many public servants leave electrical appliances and gadgets such as televisions, air conditioners, laptops, printers, lighting and other appliances on when they are not in use. Some make use of other high consuming appliances like microwaves and electric kettles at the slightest chance even when it is not necessary.

It is everyone’s responsibility to use energy as efficiently as possible and this should be an obligation on every public servant wherever they find themselves. In addition, most government building have very old and inefficient appliances that consume so much energy within the buildings. These appliances can be replaced with efficient ones to cut down on the energy consumption of public buildings. This also includes servicing this appliances as at when due.

Other measures include installing locally-produced light sensors within every space in the building and also encouraging the use of daylighting in spaces where there is enough illumination during the day. The use of energy meters to measure, monitor and control energy usage is also very important. We cannot control what we do not measure. Government employees should be educated and re-orientated about energy use. Awareness campaigns on radio, TV, newspapers should also be carried out aggressively. Energy managers should be employed in each agency to ensure compliance on energy efficiency and conservation.

If the general public visit public buildings and see that energy is being utilised efficiently with the support of awareness campaigns by media houses, social media and the government, then maybe asking Nigerians to replicate this would not be a problem.
