I Better Pass My Neighbour

  • @naijaenergyman
  • Jan 1, 2020

In Nigeria, this is one of the most used phrases in terms of power generation. It is usually used to refer to a certain type of fossil fuel based generator. As much as I intend to write from the energy perspective, it’s almost impossible to ignore the multi-dimensional perspective to the phrase.

“I better pass my neighbour” is the pidgin translation of “I am better than my neighbour”. In every typical household, street, estate, institution and community, there is the tendency for competition, the insane push to try and be better than the next person. Competition is good but then it depends on whether it is a healthy one or not. I will liken healthy competition to an invention – healthy because inventions mean someone has got to make something better than what was previously available. This would easily apply to nations that invent, design and manufacture (assuming all inventions were for good uses only).

In my country however, there is more of unhealthy competition and I will explain succinctly with a simple example. There are housing estates worth billions of naira without a drainage system. Every new building that comes up is looking to be better, more magnificent and more expensive than the rest. Noteworthy is the fact that the residents ride exotic cars worth millions of naira while ignoring the drainage worth a token of their fortune. When there is heavy rainfall, the streets get flooded and my affluent friends are forced to either park their cars in the garage or risk an engine knock and destruction of the lovely interior of their cars. They are clad in suits and yet have to wear rain boots up to their knees; their children skip school until the water recedes. Sometimes even their high and lofty jeeps are swallowed in the torrential rain.

From the energy perspective, most of these houses have 2 large generators that are usually not less than 50kVA combined and then you wonder, to power what and why? Some generating sets are so large they can power the entire estate or community and yet they are lined up in each building. Yes, they are silent generators most times but why would I want to outdo my neighbour by using a generating set 10 to 15 times more than the peak energy consumption of my household and ignoring the environmental and cost implications. These portray the scenarios common in the Victoria Island – Lekki – Ajah areas of Lagos State.

For the middle class, it is common to own more than one generator (one for sleeping overnight and one for normal daily activities). For the lower class, in a bungalow of 15 face-me-i-slap-you rooms, there are likely 15 or more I-better-pass-my-neighbour generators. These generators are not usually more than 1.5kVA but their cumulative effect of pollution and carbon emission has sometimes sent families to their early graves due to the deadly fumes. There is also noise and disregard as to where each generating set should be located.

Due to the epileptic and unstable power supply, “I better pass my neighbour” is the name given to these generators depicting that you are at least able to light up your home in the midst of blackouts whereas the next door neighbour doesn’t own one and uses candles and kerosene lanterns instead.

I hope that one day “I better pass my neighbour” will be measured by how much solar panels I have on my rooftop or how much kilowatt hour of energy I can sell back to the grid or how much I have been able to conserve energy or use it efficiently and responsibly.

Ultimately, I hope that one day citizens of this great country Nigeria would not need to generate their own electricity. I hope it will be normal to wake in the morning or sleep at night or get back from work and still have power supply.
