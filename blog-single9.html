Re: LIGHTS OUT IN NIGERIA

  • John Doe
  • Jan 1, 2020
  • 12 Comments

Dear Chimamanda,

Lovely write up on “Lights out in Nigeria” published in the New York Times and congratulations on the number of readership the article received.

It is important to let you know that tens of millions of Nigerians go through the same situation and as much as I agree 100% with all the points raised in your article, there are a few things and more I wish Nigerians knew. It is sad already that Nigerians have to generate their own electricity from the diesel and fuel generators as well as the inverter-battery system. It is equally quite sad that Nigerians have formed some bad energy usage habits which I think has been neglected and should also be addressed, and that, beyond just the failure of the existing power grid or electricity supply in Nigeria.

You talked about the cable of the Air Conditioner in your guest room melting and almost on fire. This is a very common phenomenon suffered surprisingly by the middle and upper class. The household gadgets and appliances of this class of people are usually energy vampires. They are referred to this because of their ability to draw so much power (reactive power) or useless power to say the least. The standard size and quality of the cable that should be used for these energy vampires are usually not adhered to. As a result, the cables melt as their tensile strength bulk under pressure. The practice interestingly is the use of inferior cables either knowingly by installers who want to reduce cost or unknowingly by unskilled and untrained installers. This has been one of the major causes of electrical fires from energy vampires especially, including yours, Dear Chimamanda.

I sleep and wake up every morning to the noisy clattering of my three neighbours generators. I peep through my window as they add fuel and additives to reduce the rate at which the generator consumes the fuel. But, surprisingly, they use 100Watt incandescent bulbs (usually yellow globes), sometimes the 200Watts, leave all of their lights ON and other appliances even when they are not using them. What is the point in putting additives to the fuel and still use so much energy in terms of inefficient gadgets and bad energy usage habit.

It is interesting to note from your article, that as soon as your diesel generator developed a fault, you were able to downsize your load and adjust where you had to work from and all that. I know it must have been so uncomfortable trying to adjust to the new change, but I’m sure you got over that with time. More interesting is the fact that you realize that the inverter system had its own limits.

What happened to our own hospitals making use of stored energy from solar PV systems for emergencies when there is power failure from the grid? It is sad that like you pointed, millions of Nigerians have lost their lives, studied in poor conditions, not been able to run small businesses and in your own words I will say “what brilliance stillborn”.

So as I reply your wonderful article, I believe that as a renowned and loved writer, the experience you share is the experience of millions of Nigerians. I hope that with this reply, lots of Nigerians can eventually liberate themselves and develop the right habits. We may point fingers, but there are three other fingers pointing right back at us. Yes, it is not right that we provide our own energy. Still, we can save energy, Use energy efficient appliances, Turn off electrical gadgets when they are not in use, Fix leaking water pipes and Avoid fire outbreaks by using the right size of cables for the right appliance.

May Our Lights never dim.

Yours sincerely,
Nna Chimaobi
